02 — CERTIFICATION GUIDE

The real certification that exists today

There is currently one widely recognized, vendor-issued, individually-earnable quantum computing certification. Being specific about that — rather than implying a broad landscape that doesn't exist yet — matters here.

IBM Certified Quantum Computation using Qiskit v2.X — Developer, Associate

DetailWhat to know
Exam codeC1000-179 — "Fundamentals of Quantum Computing Using Qiskit v2.X Developer"
Format60 questions, delivered through Pearson VUE testing centers or online proctoring
CostApproximately $150–$200 USD, varies by location — confirm current pricing at registration
ExpirationDoes not expire once earned
Track recordOver 1,300 people certified since the program launched in 2021, across 71 countries (figure as of the prior exam version)
CredentialDigital badge issued via Credly, plus the formal certification title
HistoryThe exam has been updated as Qiskit itself evolved — the previous version (v0.2X, exam C1000-112) was retired September 30, 2025, replaced by the current v2.X version covering Qiskit Runtime
What it actually tests: the mathematics of quantum information for single and multi-qubit systems, quantum circuit construction, core concepts like the Bloch sphere and Bell states, and practical Qiskit and Qiskit Runtime usage for creating and executing programs on IBM's simulators and real hardware. IBM's official exam page publishes the current detailed topic breakdown and a downloadable study guide — check there for the authoritative, current version rather than a summary here, since exam content is updated as Qiskit evolves.
Recommended, not required, preparation: IBM explicitly does not require any specific course before sitting the exam. An optional path many candidates use: build real circuit-writing experience first (see the Hands-On Labs section), then use IBM's free assessment exam through Pearson VUE to gauge readiness before booking the real one.

Beyond this one certification: the adjacent-credential strategy

Because a broad landscape of quantum-specific certifications doesn't exist yet, the practical approach for most specializations is pairing quantum learning with an adjacent, established certification in a related field:

Target specializationAdjacent certification strategy
Quantum machine learningAn AI/ML certification combined with QML-focused coursework — covers the hybrid workflows that dominate real quantum ML work today.
Quantum-safe security / PQCA cybersecurity or applied cryptography certification — directly useful for PQC migration work, which is a software and protocol discipline more than a quantum-hardware one.
Quantum hardware engineeringTypically requires formal physics or electrical engineering credentials (see Career Paths) rather than a short-form certification.

07 — COMMUNITY & REALISTIC TIMELINES

Where to go next

Community and events

Qiskit Slack community Cirq community (Google) Q2B conference APS March Meeting / DAMOP

Hackathons are one of the highest-leverage ways to build both skills and a portfolio quickly — annual events run by Xanadu (QHack) and MIT (iQuHACK) are well-established examples; confirm current dates, since hackathon schedules shift year to year.

If you're job-hunting specifically, not just company-building

The Quantum Economic Development Consortium (QED-C) is a company-membership body, not a job board for individuals — but its member companies post real openings on their own career pages, and dedicated quantum job boards (such as quantumjobs.us) aggregate listings specifically from this industry rather than general tech postings, which cuts down on noise considerably.

A realistic timeline, sourced rather than guessed: for the software-development path specifically, someone with a relevant existing background — programming experience, some math — moving through consistent daily study can realistically reach job offers within roughly 12 to 18 months. That figure is real and gets cited often, but it describes a specific, favorable starting point, not a universal guarantee. Hardware engineering and algorithm research paths typically run longer and often expect graduate-level credentials, covered in the Career Paths section above.
